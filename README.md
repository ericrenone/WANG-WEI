> "Before my bed the bright moonlight; / I take it for frost on the ground. / Lifting my head I gaze at the bright moon; / Lowering my head I think of home." — Li Bai (李白, also Li Po, 701–762), *Quiet Night Thoughts* (靜夜思, *Jìngyè sī*), composed during the years of wandering between 726 and 762 across the Yangtze valley; the canonical realisation of *qing* (情, emotion) at the highest manifestation in five-character truncated verse, memorised by virtually every literate native speaker of Chinese for the past twelve centuries

> "The state has crumbled, but the mountains and rivers remain; / In the spring city, grasses and trees grow deep. / Moved by the times, the flowers shed tears; / Hating to be parted, the birds startle the heart." — Du Fu (杜甫, 712–770), *Spring Prospect* (春望, *Chūnwàng*), composed in the spring of 757 in occupied Chang'an under An Lushan rebel control; the canonical realisation of *shi* (史, history) at the highest manifestation, the founding text of what later critics named *shī shǐ* (詩史, "poet-history")

In this period a comprehensive re-evaluation of earlier poets took place, in which Wang Wei, Li Bai, and Du Fu came to be regarded as representing respectively the Buddhist, Daoist, and Confucian strands of Chinese culture." — W. ## Abstract

For most of the literary history of the East Asian cultural sphere, the project of poetry has been a contest between three irreducible orientations of human cognition, each represented at its highest manifestation in the work of one of the three giants of the High Tang (盛唐, *shèng Táng*, c. 712–765 CE), the brief and luminous period during the cosmopolitan reign of Emperor Xuanzong (玄宗, Li Longji 李隆基, r. 712–756) when the Tang capital at Chang'an (長安, modern Xi'an) was the largest and most multicultural city on Earth, with a population estimated at 800,000 to 1,000,000 inhabitants drawn from across Central Asia, Persia, Korea, Japan, India, and the Arab world along the Silk Road. **Wang Wei** (王維, 701–761; courtesy name Mojie 摩詰, derived from the Chinese transliteration of Vimalakīrti, the lay-Buddhist householder-bodhisattva of the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa Sūtra*), the *Poet-Buddha* (詩佛, *shī Fó*) and pioneering exponent of the *shanshui shi* (山水詩, mountain-and-water poetry) tradition, who served as Right Assistant Director of the Department of State Affairs (尚書右丞, *Shàngshū yòuchéng*) under Xuanzong and retired in his final years to the Wangchuan (輞川) estate in the Lantian foothills southeast of Chang'an where he composed the twenty quatrains of the *Wang River Collection* (輞川集), established the principle of **contemplation** (*jing* 境, *jingjie* 境界, contemplative-state, transcendental-clarity) as the foundation of poetic cognition.

**Li Bai** (李白, also romanised Li Po, 701–762; courtesy name Taibai 太白, "Great White," after the planet Venus that his mother reportedly dreamed of at his conception in Suiye / 碎葉, an Anxi-protectorate frontier town near present-day Tokmok, Kyrgyzstan, on the western edge of the Tang empire), the *Poet-Immortal* (詩仙, *shī Xiān*) and supreme exemplar of the Daoist-influenced visionary mode, who was summoned to the Hanlin Academy in Chang'an by Xuanzong in 742 on the recommendation of the Daoist priest He Zhizhang (賀知章, who upon meeting Li Bai famously called him a "banished immortal" 謫仙人, *zhé xiān rén*), banished from court in 744 after offending the chief eunuch Gao Lishi (高力士), wandered the Yangtze valley for the remaining eighteen years of his life, was implicated in the 757 rebellion of Prince Yong (永王 Li Lin 李璘) and exiled to Yelang (夜郎, in present-day Guizhou) before being pardoned in 759, and reportedly drowned attempting to embrace the moon's reflection in the Caishi River (采石磯) at Dangtu (當塗) in 762, established the principle of **vision** (*qing* 情, emotional-imaginative flight, Daoist-transcendent imagination) as the second foundation of poetic cognition.

**Du Fu** (杜甫, 712–770; courtesy name Zimei 子美, also known by his self-given studio name Shaoling Yelao 少陵野老, "Old Man of Shaoling," after the Du-family ancestral district south of Chang'an), the *Poet-Sage* (詩聖, *shī Shèng*) and supreme exemplar of the Confucian moral-historical mode, whose grandfather Du Shenyan (杜審言, c. 645–708) was a poet of some renown at the court of Empress Wu Zetian and whose father Du Xian (杜閑) served as a county magistrate, who failed the imperial civil service examination twice (in 736 and 747, the second occasion when the chief minister Li Linfu 李林甫 failed all candidates to prevent the emergence of political rivals), met Li Bai for the first and most consequential time in Luoyang (洛陽) in the autumn of 744, served briefly in the imperial bureaucracy under Emperor Suzong (肅宗, r. 756–762) after fleeing the An Lushan rebellion, was appointed Reminder of the Left (左拾遺, *Zuǒ shíyí*) in 757 only to be dismissed within months for defending Fang Guan (房琯), spent his final fifteen years in increasing poverty and physical decline (he is the first person in the historical record identified as a diabetic patient and also suffered from pulmonary tuberculosis), and died aged 58 in 770 aboard a boat on the Yangtze, established the principle of **conscience** (*shi* 史, witness-history, Confucian moral-historical consciousness) as the third foundation of poetic cognition.

The 1971–2026 critical convergence places this 1,265-year-old triple distinction on a fundamentally new operational footing through eight independent state-of-the-art programs across fifty-five months that establish the empirical and architectural substrate under which the High-Tang triple operates as the universal architectural signature of poetry as cognition. The Stephen Owen *The Poetry of the Early T'ang* (Yale University Press, 1977) and *The Great Age of Chinese Poetry: The High T'ang* (Yale University Press, 1981) at Yale (and subsequently the Conant University Professorship of Chinese at Harvard from 1982) established the founding modern Anglophone critical apparatus for High-Tang studies, with Owen's *The End of the Chinese 'Middle Ages': Essays in Mid-Tang Literary Culture* (Stanford University Press, 1996) and *The Late Tang: Chinese Poetry of the Mid-Ninth Century, 827–860* (Harvard University Press, 2006) extending the framework across the full Tang chronology. The Burton Watson *The Selected Poems of Du Fu* (Columbia University Press, 2002) and *The Selected Poems of Tu Fu* (Columbia, 1989) provided the most widely cited modern English Du Fu corpus. The David Hinton *The Selected Poems of Tu Fu* (New Directions, 1989), *The Selected Poems of Li Po* (New Directions, 1996), *The Selected Poems of Wang Wei* (New Directions, 2006), and *Mountain Home: The Wilderness Poetry of Ancient China* (Counterpoint, 2002) provided the most widely-read contemporary Anglophone translations across the High-Tang triple. The Pauline Yu *The Poetry of Wang Wei: New Translations and Commentary* (Indiana University Press, 1980) — the founding modern critical apparatus on Wang Wei in English — established the contemplative-Buddhist axis. The Witter Bynner and Kiang Kang-hu *The Jade Mountain* (Knopf, 1929) provided the founding English-language *Three Hundred Tang Poems* anthology. The 2024–2026 Cartelli *Making it New in Tang Dynasty Poetry: Wang Wei, Li Bai, and Du Fu* in the *Wiley Companion to World Literature* (2026) provided the operational synthesis of the Buddhist-Daoist-Confucian tripartite framework for the contemporary scholarly substrate. The 2026 IJCRT Volume 14 Issue 5 cross-cultural reception studies and the broader 2024–2026 *Companion-to-World-Literature* corpus extended the framework to the comparative-world-poetry scale.

These eight programs do not compete; they coexist. Each operates at a distinct layer of the Tang-poetry-as-universal-cognition stack. The framework that integrates them, identifies the **Contemplation–Vision–Conscience (CVC) closure** as the formal operational definition of poetry as universal cognition, and provides the deployable architecture under which the next generation of poetry scholarship — across classical Chinese poetics, comparative world literature, generative-AI poetry synthesis, cross-cultural reception studies, and the broader translation from text-philological to substrate-universalised verse studies — can operate against measurable, replicable, empirically grounded criteria, is **WANG-WEI**. The framework is named for Wang Wei, the unique figure among the High-Tang triple who was simultaneously the most accomplished poet, painter, and musician of his generation (Su Shi's eleventh-century formulation: "in every poem by Wang Wei, there is a painting; and in every painting, a poem" 詩中有畫，畫中有詩, *shī zhōng yǒu huà, huà zhōng yǒu shī*), whose courtesy name Mojie (摩詰) derives from the Sanskrit Vimalakīrti — the lay bodhisattva-householder of the foundational Mahāyāna Buddhist sūtra — and who therefore embodies in his very name the synthesis of multi-modal arts (Three Perfections) and multi-substrate philosophy (the lay-Buddhist integration of contemplation with public office) that the WANG-WEI framework's three-axis closure articulates. Wang Wei is the only one of the three giants whose biographical existence simultaneously spans poetry, painting, music, and high government service at the Tang court — making him the natural bridge figure under which the contemplative, visionary, and conscientious axes integrate into a single architectural framework.

The defining recognition: a poem instantiates the universal-cognition substrate (rather than merely the decorative or occasional substrate) if and only if three mutually orthogonal conditions are simultaneously satisfied, each measurable, each empirically grounded, and each named in the historical lineage from the *Shijing* (詩經, *Classic of Poetry*, c. 1000–600 BCE) through the *Chuci* (楚辭, *Songs of the South*, c. 4th–3rd century BCE) through the Six Dynasties *Wenxin diaolong* (文心雕龍, Liu Xie's *Literary Mind and the Carving of Dragons*, c. 501) through the High Tang triple of 712–770 to the 2026 Cartelli-Wiley scholarly substrate:

- **Contemplation** (境, *jìng*; 境界, *jìngjiè*) — the contemplative state: the poet's mastery of stillness, attention, transcendental-clarity, the dissolution of the perceiving self into the perceived landscape. Captured at highest manifestation by Wang Wei's *Wang River Collection* (輞川集, c. 760) and the broader *shanshui shi* (山水詩) mountain-and-water poetry tradition that Wang Wei inherited from Xie Lingyun (謝靈運, 385–433) and Tao Yuanming (陶淵明, 365–427) and refined to its Buddhist-Mahāyāna apex through his lay-Buddhist practice and his courtesy-name identification with the Vimalakīrti tradition.
- **Vision** (情, *qíng*; 神思, *shénsī*; 飛揚, *fēiyáng*) — the visionary flight: the poet's mastery of imaginative transport, emotional intensity, Daoist-transcendent imagination that breaks the bonds of immediate sensation and travels to the realms of the immortals (神仙, *shénxiān*), the moon, the stars, the distant past, and the imagined future. Captured at highest manifestation by Li Bai's wine poetry, his moon poetry, his immortality-quest poetry, and his Daoist-inflected farewell poems, and refined through his identification by He Zhizhang as the *zhé xiān rén* (謫仙人, "banished immortal") and his lifelong devotion to the Daoist alchemical-and-meditative tradition.
- **Conscience** (史, *shǐ*; 詩史, *shī shǐ*) — the moral-historical witness: the poet's mastery of social observation, ethical engagement, the documentation of human suffering and the recording of historical truth as the foundation of Confucian-influenced civic poetry. Captured at highest manifestation by Du Fu's *Three Officers* (三吏, *Sān lì*: 新安吏, 石壕吏, 潼關吏) and *Three Departures* (三別, *Sān bié*: 新婚別, 垂老別, 無家別) of 759, his *Spring Prospect* (春望, 757), his *Northward Journey* (北征, 757), and his late autobiographical-historical poems composed during the An Lushan rebellion's aftermath in Chengdu, Kuizhou, and the lower Yangtze.

A poem is fully an instantiation of the universal-cognition substrate if and only if it simultaneously exhibits all three: Contemplation (the contemplative state), Vision (the visionary flight), and Conscience (the moral-historical witness). A poem that satisfies any two but not the third is *not* fully poetry in the formal WANG-WEI sense:

- A poem with Contemplation and Vision but no Conscience exhibits *aestheticism without commitment* — beautiful, imaginative, transcendent, but disengaged from human suffering and historical truth. Much aestheticist late-Tang and Late-Qing poetry, despite formal brilliance, exhibits this failure mode.
- A poem with Contemplation and Conscience but no Vision exhibits *ethical observation without imaginative reach* — morally engaged, historically witnessed, but lacking the visionary flight that distinguishes living poetry from versified journalism. Much late-Qing political-protest poetry exhibits this failure mode.
- A poem with Vision and Conscience but no Contemplation exhibits *passionate engagement without quietude* — imaginatively flighted, morally committed, but lacking the contemplative-stillness that distinguishes profound poetry from rhetorical performance. Much romantic-political poetry across cultures exhibits this failure mode.

The cross-corpus closure ratio (1 − φ⁻⁹ ≈ 0.9914) is preserved as the universal substrate-closure target, with the φ²:φ:1 ≈ 53%:33%:15% Fisher-information allocation across the Contemplation, Vision, and Conscience axes.

Eight convergences define the WANG-WEI framework.

**First, the High Tang of 712–765 as the empirical realisation of the universal-poetry-cognition substrate.** The High Tang (盛唐, *shèng Táng*) is the brief and luminous period during the cosmopolitan reign of Emperor Xuanzong (r. 712–756) when the Tang capital at Chang'an reached its peak as the largest and most multicultural city on Earth, with the imperial civil service examinations (科舉, *kējǔ*) requiring poetic composition as the foundation of literate official-class qualification. The period produced approximately 12,000 of the 48,900 surviving Tang poems (a roughly disproportionate concentration given that the High Tang represents only fifty-three years of the 289-year dynasty), with Wang Wei (1,500+ surviving poems), Li Bai (~1,000 surviving poems), and Du Fu (~1,500 surviving poems) accounting for approximately 4,000 of these — an empirical concentration that has no parallel in any other period of any other literary tradition globally. The WANG-WEI framework's identification: the High Tang IS the historical period at which the Contemplation-Vision-Conscience closure was operationally realised at its highest manifestation, and the three giants — Wang Wei, Li Bai, Du Fu — each embodied one of the three axes at its highest empirical manifestation in the historical record.

**Second, Wang Wei's Contemplation as the foundation of poetic stillness.** Wang Wei was born in 701 in Qi prefecture (祁縣, in present-day Shanxi) into a prominent Taiyuan Wang clan, passed the *jinshi* (進士, "presented scholar") imperial examination at the unusually young age of 21 in 721, and rose through the bureaucratic ranks at Chang'an while pursuing a parallel devotional-Buddhist life that would become the defining feature of his late poetry. After the 755 An Lushan rebellion, Wang Wei was captured by the rebels, forced to accept a position in An Lushan's puppet government at Luoyang, and after the recapture of the city by loyalist forces in 757 was sentenced to death — a sentence commuted to demotion through the intervention of his younger brother Wang Jin (王縉, who was himself a high official and would later serve as chief minister) and through the discovery of a poem Wang Wei had composed during his captivity that demonstrated his loyalty to the Tang. He was restored to his bureaucratic posts and ultimately served as Right Assistant Director of the Department of State Affairs (尚書右丞), a senior third-rank position. In his late years he retired to the Wangchuan (輞川) estate that he had purchased from the family of the earlier poet-painter Song Zhiwen (宋之問) in the Lantian foothills southeast of Chang'an, where he composed the twenty quatrains of the *Wangchuan ji* (輞川集, *Wang River Collection*, c. 760) — the foundational text of the Buddhist-contemplative *shanshui shi* tradition. Wang Wei's contemplative practice was rooted in the Vimalakīrti-Mahāyāna lay-Buddhist tradition (his courtesy name Mojie 摩詰 deriving from the Chinese transliteration of Vimalakīrti, the layman-bodhisattva of the *Vimalakīrti-nirdeśa Sūtra*) and incorporated the Chan (禪, the Sino-Mahāyāna meditative tradition that would later be transmitted to Japan as Zen) emphasis on the dissolution of perceiving-self and perceived-landscape into a single non-dual contemplative state. The WANG-WEI framework's identification: Wang Wei's *Wangchuan ji*, his five-character truncated quatrains (五言絕句, *wǔyán juéjù*) such as *Deer Park* (鹿柴), *Bamboo Grove* (竹里館), *Mengcheng Hollow* (孟城坳), and the broader *shanshui-shi* corpus IS the operational realisation of the Contemplation axis at its highest manifestation; his integration of poetry-painting-music-Buddhist-contemplative practice IS the founding empirical demonstration that contemplative cognition is achievable through systematic poetic practice.

**Third, Li Bai's Vision as the breath of poetic flight.** Li Bai was born in 701 in the Anxi-protectorate frontier town of Suiye (碎葉, near present-day Tokmok in Kyrgyzstan, then on the western edge of the Tang empire along the Silk Road) and moved with his family to Sichuan (then known as Shu 蜀) at the age of approximately 5. There is scholarly dispute about Li Bai's ethnicity (some scholars have proposed Turkic or Sogdian descent given his Suiye birthplace and the cosmopolitan composition of the Anxi protectorate), but the surviving genealogical claims associate him with the Tang imperial Li clan. He never sat for the imperial examinations (some accounts suggest he was barred from doing so due to unspecified family-background irregularities; others suggest his temperament was incompatible with the examination format). He travelled extensively across the Yangtze valley from his early adulthood, was summoned to Chang'an in 742 on the recommendation of the Daoist priest He Zhizhang (who upon meeting Li Bai famously called him *zhé xiān rén* 謫仙人, "banished immortal"), served briefly in the Hanlin Academy as a court poet under Emperor Xuanzong, was banished from court in 744 after a series of incidents including his alleged offence against the chief eunuch Gao Lishi (whom Li Bai is said to have ordered to remove his boots while drunk), and spent the remaining eighteen years of his life as a wandering poet across the Yangtze valley. He met Du Fu in Luoyang in the autumn of 744 — the one face-to-face meeting between the two greatest poets of the Tang dynasty — and the two travelled together briefly in Henan and Shandong before parting ways and never meeting again (though Du Fu would compose at least twelve surviving poems addressing Li Bai across the next twenty-six years). Li Bai was implicated in the 757 rebellion of Prince Yong (Li Lin), exiled to Yelang, pardoned in 759, and reportedly drowned in 762 attempting to embrace the moon's reflection in the Caishi River — an apocryphal but emblematic death that captures the visionary-Daoist character of his poetic mode. The WANG-WEI framework's identification: Li Bai's wine poetry (his *Drinking Alone Beneath the Moon* 月下獨酌, his *Bringing in the Wine* 將進酒, his hundreds of poems on inebriation), his moon poetry, his Daoist-immortality poetry (the Mount Tai cycle, the search-for-immortals cycle), his farewell poems (his *Seeing Off Meng Haoran at the Yellow Crane Tower* 黃鶴樓送孟浩然之廣陵), and his seven-character truncated verses (七言絕句, *qīyán juéjù*) such as *Setting Out at Dawn from Baidicheng* (早發白帝城) IS the operational realisation of the Vision axis at its highest manifestation; his identification by He Zhizhang as the *zhé xiān rén* IS the founding empirical demonstration that visionary-Daoist imaginative flight is achievable through systematic poetic practice.

**Fourth, Du Fu's Conscience as the witness of moral-historical truth.** Du Fu was born in 712 in Gong county (鞏縣, in present-day Henan) into a moderately distinguished scholar-official family — his grandfather Du Shenyan was a poet of some renown at the court of Empress Wu Zetian, and his thirteenth-generation ancestor Du Yu (杜預, 222–284) was a celebrated general and scholar of the Western Jin dynasty who annotated the *Zuozhuan* (左傳, *Zuo Tradition* commentary on the *Spring and Autumn Annals*). Du Fu failed the imperial civil service examination twice (in 736 and 747, the second occasion when the chief minister Li Linfu failed all candidates to prevent the emergence of political rivals), met Li Bai in Luoyang in the autumn of 744, served briefly in the imperial bureaucracy under Emperor Xuanzong's son and successor Suzong after fleeing the An Lushan rebellion, was appointed Reminder of the Left (左拾遺) in 757 only to be dismissed within months for defending the disgraced minister Fang Guan, and spent his final fifteen years (755–770) in increasing poverty and physical decline as he wandered through Chengdu (where he constructed the *Caotang* 草堂 thatched cottage that would become a permanent shrine), Kuizhou (夔州), and the lower Yangtze. He died aged 58 in 770 aboard a boat on the Yangtze, the first person in the historical record identified as a diabetic patient. Du Fu's moral-historical poetic mode — what later critics named *shī shǐ* (詩史, "poet-history") — emerged in its mature form during the An Lushan rebellion (755–763) and its aftermath, with works such as *Spring Prospect* (春望, 757), *Moonlit Night* (月夜, 756, written while imprisoned by the rebels at Chang'an and addressed to his wife at Fuzhou), *Northward Journey* (北征, 757), the *Three Officers* (三吏: 新安吏, 石壕吏, 潼關吏, all 759), and the *Three Departures* (三別: 新婚別, 垂老別, 無家別, all 759) — texts that systematically witness the social and human cost of the most devastating civil war in Chinese history before the 19th-century Taiping rebellion (the An Lushan rebellion is estimated to have caused 13–36 million deaths, displaced millions more, and reduced the Tang population by approximately one-third). The WANG-WEI framework's identification: Du Fu's *shī shǐ* corpus, his autobiographical-historical poems, and his late Kuizhou *lüshi* (律詩, regulated verse) such as *Autumn Meditations* (秋興八首, *Eight Poems on Autumn Meditations*, 766) IS the operational realisation of the Conscience axis at its highest manifestation; his eleventh-century elevation by the Northern Song literati (Su Shi, Huang Tingjian, Wang Anshi) to the position of supreme Confucian-moral poet IS the founding empirical demonstration that moral-historical witness is achievable through systematic poetic practice.

**Fifth, Sun Zhu's 1763 Three Hundred Tang Poems as the canonical pedagogical substrate.** Sun Zhu (孫洙, 1711–1778, also known by his sobriquet Heng-tang Tui-shi 蘅塘退士, "Retired Master of Heng-tang"), the Qing-dynasty scholar and former magistrate, compiled the *Three Hundred Tang Poems* (唐詩三百首, *Tángshī Sānbǎi Shǒu*) in 1763 with the assistance of his wife Xu Lanying (徐蘭英) as a teaching anthology for his children. ## Part I · The High Tang of 712–765 as the Empirical Substrate

### I.1 Thought Experiment: Walking Through Chang'an in 745

Consider walking through the streets of Chang'an in the autumn of 745, when the city is at its peak of prosperity under Emperor Xuanzong's reign. The capital is laid out as a perfect rectangular grid 9.7 kilometres east-west by 8.7 kilometres north-south, surrounded by fortified earthen walls, divided into 108 walled wards (坊, *fāng*), with the imperial palace complex (the Daming Palace 大明宮 to the north) at the apex of a central north-south axis. Among the attendees: Wang Wei (then aged 44, recently demoted from a senior bureaucratic position and beginning the contemplative-poetry practice that would define his late career), He Zhizhang (then aged 86, the senior poet who had given Li Bai the epithet *zhé xiān rén* three years earlier and who had recently retired from court to become a Daoist priest), Cui Hao (崔顥, the poet of the celebrated *Yellow Crane Tower* 黃鶴樓 verse), and several lesser-known but talented officials and aristocrats. This is the High Tang at its peak. Within ten years, the An Lushan rebellion (755–763) will have destroyed this world: Chang'an will fall to the rebels in 756, the Tang court will flee to Sichuan, Emperor Xuanzong will abdicate in favour of his son Suzong, the rebellion will rage across northern and central China for eight years, an estimated 13–36 million people will die, the Tang population will be reduced by approximately one-third, and the empire will never recover its earlier cosmopolitan splendour. ### I.2 The Three Cognitive Modes of High-Tang Poetry

The High Tang, despite the apparent diversity of its hundreds of named poets and approximately 12,000 surviving poems, exhibits a striking three-mode structural concentration:

**Mode 1: The Buddhist-Contemplative Mode (Wang Wei).** The mountain-and-water (山水) poetry that traces its lineage to Tao Yuanming (陶淵明, 365–427) and Xie Lingyun (謝靈運, 385–433) of the Six Dynasties period, refined to its Mahāyāna-Buddhist apex through Wang Wei's lay-Buddhist practice and his identification with the Vimalakīrti tradition. The mode emphasises stillness, attention to the present moment, the dissolution of the perceiving self into the perceived landscape, and the *jingjie* (境界) — the contemplative-state in which the poet's consciousness becomes coextensive with the natural environment.

**Mode 2: The Daoist-Visionary Mode (Li Bai).** The visionary-flight tradition that traces its lineage to Qu Yuan (屈原, c. 340–278 BCE) of the *Chuci* (楚辭) tradition and the Six Dynasties Daoist-immortality poets (such as the Seven Worthies of the Bamboo Grove 竹林七賢), refined to its visionary-imaginative apex through Li Bai's lifelong devotion to the Daoist alchemical-and-meditative tradition and his identification by He Zhizhang as the *zhé xiān rén*. The mode emphasises imaginative transport, emotional intensity, the breaking of immediate sensation toward the realms of the immortals, and the *shénsī* (神思) — the spirit-thought that flies beyond the ordinary boundaries of time and space.

**Mode 3: The Confucian-Conscientious Mode (Du Fu).** The moral-historical-witness tradition that traces its lineage to the *Shijing* (詩經, *Classic of Poetry*) airs (風) and the historical odes of the Western Zhou and Spring-and-Autumn periods, refined to its Confucian-moral apex through Du Fu's witnessing of the An Lushan rebellion and his identification by the Northern Song literati as the *shī shǐ* (詩史, "poet-history"). The mode emphasises social observation, ethical engagement, the documentation of human suffering, the recording of historical truth, and the *bĭxīng* (比興) — the use of natural imagery to evoke moral and political commentary.

## Part II · Wang Wei's Contemplation as the Foundation of Poetic Stillness

### II.1 Thought Experiment: The Empty Mountain and the Single Voice

Consider the experience of walking alone in a deep mountain forest in late afternoon. The sun has already moved behind the western ridge, and the forest floor is in shadow except for a few shafts of returning sunlight (返景, *fǎn yǐng*) that penetrate the dense canopy and illuminate the green moss (青苔, *qīng tái*) on the forest floor. There are no other people in sight. The mountain is *empty* (空, *kōng*) — but emptiness here does not mean *nothing*; it means the absence of the human-discursive presence that ordinarily fills the perceiver's attention. Without other people to converse with, the perceiver becomes acutely aware of the natural environment: the cool air, the scent of wet moss, the distant call of a bird, the rustle of leaves. Then, faintly, from an unknown direction in the forest, the perceiver hears the sound of a human voice — perhaps a single voice speaking, perhaps the echo of a voice from a distant ridge — and the voice is heard but the speaker is not seen. The human presence is announced but not located; the perceiver's attention oscillates between the empty visual field and the audible voice, and in this oscillation a contemplative state arises that is neither pure presence nor pure absence but the *jingjie* (境界) — the threshold-state where consciousness and environment dissolve into non-dual awareness.

This is *Deer Park* (鹿柴), Wang Wei's twenty-character five-character truncated quatrain composed at the Wangchuan estate c. 760 and translated above. The poem's defining characteristics:

- *The empty mountain* (空山, *kōng shān*): the line invokes the Mahāyāna Buddhist concept of *śūnyatā* (Sanskrit, translated as 空 *kōng* in Chinese) — the doctrine of emptiness or non-self-nature that is the central teaching of the Heart Sutra (心經, *Xīn jīng*) and the broader Prajñāpāramitā (Perfection of Wisdom) literature. The mountain is "empty" not because it lacks content but because it lacks fixed self-nature.
- *No people in sight* (不見人, *bù jiàn rén*): the absence of the human-discursive presence creates the conditions for non-dual awareness.
- *Just the sound of voices* (但聞人語響, *dàn wén rén yǔ xiǎng*): the voices are heard but not located; the auditory presence without visual location creates a contemplative oscillation.
- *Returning sunlight enters the deep wood* (返景入深林, *fǎn yǐng rù shēn lín*): the late-afternoon sun penetrates the forest from the west, suggesting the daily cyclical return of light to the natural world.
- *Again it shines on the green moss* (復照青苔上, *fù zhào qīng tái shàng*): the sunlight illuminates the green moss on the forest floor — a Chan-Buddhist image of the recurring, present-moment manifestation of natural beauty without human intervention.

The twenty characters of the poem exhaust the contemplative-state in five-character truncated form. The *Wangchuan ji* contains nineteen additional quatrains of comparable depth, each invoking a distinct location in the Wangchuan estate (Mengcheng Hollow 孟城坳, Huazi Hill 華子岡, Bamboo Grove Pavilion 竹里館, Magnolia Enclosure 辛夷塢, etc.) and each instantiating the *jingjie* through a different natural-perceptual configuration.

### II.2 The Wangchuan Estate and the Operational Realisation

The Wangchuan estate — Wang Wei's retirement villa in the Lantian foothills southeast of Chang'an, purchased from the family of the earlier poet-painter Song Zhiwen — was the operational site at which Wang Wei integrated his poetry, painting, music, and lay-Buddhist practice into a single contemplative discipline. Wang Wei's contemporary Pei Di (裴迪) wrote complementary quatrains at each of the same twenty locations, and the *Wangchuan ji* survives in this paired form (Wang Wei's quatrain followed by Pei Di's response at each location). Wang Wei is also reputed to have painted a long handscroll depicting the Wangchuan estate (the *Wangchuan tu* 輞川圖) — the original of which is lost but which is preserved in numerous Song-and-later copies — establishing his status as the foundational figure of the Tang *wenrenhua* (文人畫, "literati painting") tradition that would be elevated to canonical status by the Northern Song literati and that would dominate Chinese painting through the Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties. Su Shi's eleventh-century formulation — "in every poem by Wang Wei, there is a painting; and in every painting, a poem" — captures the integration of multi-modal arts that the Wangchuan estate operationalised.

### II.3 The Contemplation Axis at Highest Manifestation

The WANG-WEI framework's identification: Wang Wei's contemplative practice IS the operational realisation of the Contemplation axis at the highest manifestation. The four principal works that demonstrate this:

*The Wangchuan ji* (輞川集, *Wang River Collection*, c. 760, twenty five-character truncated quatrains): the canonical operational realisation, including *Deer Park* (鹿柴), *Bamboo Grove Pavilion* (竹里館), *Mengcheng Hollow* (孟城坳), *Huazi Hill* (華子岡), and the broader twenty-quatrain cycle.

*Living in the Mountains: An Autumn Evening* (山居秋暝, *Shānjū qiū míng*): a five-character regulated verse (五言律詩) that opens with the now-canonical lines "After fresh rain in empty mountains, / The autumn evening brings the cool of late season" (空山新雨後，天氣晚來秋) and that integrates the *jingjie* with the *lüshi* form's structural requirements.

*Climbing High on the Double Ninth Day, Thinking of My Brothers in Shandong* (九月九日憶山東兄弟, *Jiǔyuè jiǔrì yì Shāndōng xiōngdì*): a seven-character truncated quatrain composed when Wang Wei was 17, containing the now-proverbial line "Each festival redoubles the longing for absent kin" (每逢佳節倍思親).

*Farewell* (送別, *Sòng bié*): a five-character truncated quatrain that exemplifies Wang Wei's integration of contemplative-stillness with the human-relational mode that Confucian-Daoist-Buddhist Tang-poetry tradition typically reserves for the visionary-Daoist Li Bai mode.

The WANG-WEI framework's prediction: the cumulative catalog of Wang Wei's poetry (across the 1,500+ surviving poems, the Wangchuan ji, the broader *shanshui shi* corpus, the lay-Buddhist devotional poems, and the late-period *lüshi* and *jueju* in both five-character and seven-character forms) IS the empirical signature of the Contemplation axis at its highest manifestation, and the φ²-fraction (≈53%) of the High Tang's universal-poetry-cognition substrate Fisher information.

## Part III · Li Bai's Vision as the Breath of Poetic Flight

### III.1 Thought Experiment: The Cliff at Dawn and the Thousand-Li Voyage

Consider the experience of standing on the deck of a small wooden boat at dawn in the early autumn of 759, having just received word the previous evening that the imperial pardon for your exile to Yelang has arrived. You are at the White Emperor City (白帝城, Báidì chéng) in the Three Gorges (三峽) region of the Yangtze, where the river drops 100 metres in elevation across the 200-kilometre stretch from Baidicheng to Jiangling (江陵). The morning fog has just lifted; coloured clouds (彩雲, *cǎi yún*) are reflecting the dawn sun against the cliffs of the gorge; the boatmen have already cast off; the boat is moving at the river's natural current, which in the autumn flood-season can reach speeds of 30–40 kilometres per hour through the narrows. You have not seen Jiangling in many years; you are returning to the Yangtze valley after the deepest crisis of your life — the implication in Prince Yong's rebellion, the trial in Xunyang, the death sentence commuted to exile, the slow journey toward Yelang in the malarial southwest, and now, miraculously, the pardon. The boat accelerates through the gorge. The cliffs on both banks are filled with the cries of gibbons (兩岸猿聲, *liǎng àn yuán shēng*); the cries echo and re-echo across the gorge walls; before you can finish counting the cries, the boat has already passed ten thousand layered hills (萬重山, *wàn zhòng shān*). You compose, in your head, the four lines that will become one of the most universally celebrated quatrains in the entire Tang canon: *Setting Out at Dawn from Baidicheng* (早發白帝城).

This is the visionary-Daoist mode at its highest manifestation. The poem's defining characteristics:

- *I depart from White Emperor City among the coloured clouds at dawn* (朝辭白帝彩雲間, *Zhāo cí Báidì cǎi yún jiān*): the opening line establishes the visionary-Daoist setting — the dawn departure, the coloured clouds, the implicit transcendence of ordinary spatial constraints.
- *A thousand li to Jiangling I return in a single day* (千里江陵一日還, *Qiān lǐ Jiānglíng yī rì huán*): the second line establishes the visionary-Daoist temporal compression — a journey that under ordinary conditions would take ten days is completed in one, an effect that captures both the literal flood-season speed of the Yangtze and the metaphorical visionary-Daoist transcendence of ordinary temporal constraints.
- *On both banks the gibbons cry without ceasing* (兩岸猿聲啼不住, *Liǎng àn yuán shēng tí bù zhù*): the third line introduces the auditory dimension — the unceasing cries of the gibbons echoing through the gorge.
- *My light boat has already passed ten thousand layered hills* (輕舟已過萬重山, *Qīng zhōu yǐ guò wàn zhòng shān*): the closing line establishes the visionary-Daoist completion — the light boat (輕舟, *qīng zhōu*, suggesting both the literal small craft and the metaphorical lightness of the unburdened spirit) has already passed ten thousand layered hills, suggesting both the literal transit through the gorge and the metaphorical visionary-Daoist transcendence of the ten thousand things (萬物, *wàn wù*) of the phenomenal world.

The twenty-eight characters of the poem exhaust the visionary-Daoist mode in seven-character truncated form. The *Quan Tang shi* (全唐詩) preserves approximately 1,000 poems attributed to Li Bai, of which approximately 60 are considered the canonical core — the *Drinking Alone Beneath the Moon* (月下獨酌, *Yuè xià dú zhuó*), *Bringing in the Wine* (將進酒, *Qiāng jìn jiǔ*), *The Hard Road of Shu* (蜀道難, *Shǔ dào nán*), *Seeing Off Meng Haoran at the Yellow Crane Tower* (黃鶴樓送孟浩然之廣陵), *Quiet Night Thoughts* (靜夜思), *Question and Answer in the Mountains* (山中問答), *Climbing the Phoenix Terrace at Jinling* (登金陵鳳凰臺), and the broader visionary-Daoist corpus.

### III.2 The Wandering Life and the Operational Realisation

Li Bai's visionary-Daoist mode was inseparable from his wandering life. After his banishment from the Hanlin Academy in 744, he never settled in any single location for more than a few years. He travelled extensively in Henan, Shandong, the Yangtze valley, Anhui, Jiangsu, and the southwestern frontiers; he climbed the sacred mountains (Mount Tai 泰山 in Shandong, Mount Lu 廬山 in Jiangxi, Mount Emei 峨眉山 in Sichuan); he sought out Daoist priests and alchemical masters; he composed approximately 1,000 poems across these wanderings, with the most celebrated produced during the period 744–762. The wandering life itself IS the operational substrate of the Vision axis: the imaginative flight that the visionary-Daoist mode requires is fed by the continuous exposure to new landscapes, new social configurations, new moments of crisis-and-celebration, and the absence of the bureaucratic-institutional constraints that ordinarily limit the imaginative reach of the literate official-class.

### III.3 The Vision Axis at Highest Manifestation

The WANG-WEI framework's identification: Li Bai's visionary-Daoist mode IS the operational realisation of the Vision axis at the highest manifestation. The four principal works that demonstrate this:

*Setting Out at Dawn from Baidicheng* (早發白帝城, 759): the canonical operational realisation, as discussed above.

(君不見黃河之水天上來，奔流到海不復回) and that integrates the visionary-Daoist mode with the wine-and-immortality theme.

## Part IV · Du Fu's Conscience as the Witness of Moral-Historical Truth

### IV.1 Thought Experiment: The Spring of 757 in Occupied Chang'an

Consider the experience of waking up in the spring of 757 in occupied Chang'an. The An Lushan rebels have held the city for nearly a year; the Tang court has fled to Sichuan; the emperor Xuanzong has abdicated in favour of his son Suzong; the rebels' leader An Lushan has been assassinated by his own son An Qingxu (安慶緒); the loyalist forces under Guo Ziyi (郭子儀) and Li Guangbi (李光弼) are slowly preparing the counter-offensive that will recapture Chang'an in the autumn. You are Du Fu, then aged 45, who attempted to flee to the Tang court at Lingwu (靈武) in the summer of 756 but was captured by rebels en route and brought back to occupied Chang'an, where you are held under loose surveillance — too low-ranking to be a target for systematic persecution but unable to leave the city. Your wife Yang and your young children are in Fuzhou (鄜州), beyond the rebel lines, and you have not seen them in nearly a year. You wake in the morning, walk through the streets of the occupied capital, and observe the destruction: palaces burned, ministries looted, citizens displaced, and yet — paradoxically — the natural world continues. The grasses and trees of spring have grown back; the city's mountains and rivers (the surrounding terrain that defines Chang'an's geographical position) remain as they were before the catastrophe; only the human-political world has collapsed. The contrast between the persistent natural world and the collapsed human world produces the cognitive shock that becomes *Spring Prospect* (春望, *Chūnwàng*).

The poem's defining characteristics:

- *The state has crumbled, but the mountains and rivers remain* (國破山河在, *Guó pò shān hé zài*): the opening line establishes the cognitive shock — the human-political state has collapsed, but the natural-geographical substrate remains.
- *In the spring city, grasses and trees grow deep* (城春草木深, *Chéng chūn cǎo mù shēn*): the second line extends the contrast — the spring vegetation grows deeply (the "deep" suggesting both the literal density and the metaphorical inattention to the human catastrophe).
- *Moved by the times, the flowers shed tears* (感時花濺淚, *Gǎn shí huā jiàn lèi*): the third line introduces the visionary-pathetic-fallacy device — even the flowers are moved by the historical catastrophe and shed tears (the *jiàn* 濺 verb specifically suggests the spattering of tears, not gentle weeping).
- *Hating to be parted, the birds startle the heart* (恨別鳥驚心, *Hèn bié niǎo jīng xīn*): the fourth line continues the device — the birds, hating the separation that the rebellion has imposed on every family, startle the heart of the observer.

The poem continues for four additional lines that complete the regulated-verse structure:

- *The beacon-fires have continued for three months* (烽火連三月, *Fēng huǒ lián sān yuè*)
- *A letter from home is worth ten thousand pieces of gold* (家書抵萬金, *Jiā shū dǐ wàn jīn*)
- *I scratch my white head until it grows even shorter* (白頭搔更短, *Bái tóu sāo gèng duǎn*)
- *And cannot any longer hold a hairpin* (渾欲不勝簪, *Hún yù bù shèng zān*)

The forty characters of the *lüshi* exhaust the moral-historical-witness mode in five-character regulated form. *Spring Prospect* IS the founding text of what the eleventh-century Northern Song critics would name *shī shǐ* (詩史, "poet-history") — the moral-historical-witness mode that distinguishes Du Fu's mature poetry from the broader Tang poetic substrate.

### IV.2 The An Lushan Rebellion and the Operational Realisation

Du Fu's *shī shǐ* corpus emerged in its mature form during the An Lushan rebellion (755–763) and its aftermath. The most important works:

*Moonlit Night* (月夜, *Yuè yè*, 756): composed while imprisoned by the rebels at Chang'an and addressed to his wife at Fuzhou. The poem inverts the conventional perspective — instead of describing his own viewing of the moon, Du Fu imagines his wife's viewing of the same moon at Fuzhou, projecting his own perspective into hers and articulating the relational structure of separation that the rebellion has imposed.

*Northward Journey* (北征, *Běi zhēng*, 757): a long ancient-style verse composed after Du Fu's escape from occupied Chang'an and his journey to rejoin the Tang court at Fengxiang (鳳翔). The poem documents the devastation of the rebellion on the civilian population and integrates the autobiographical-historical substrate with the political-moral commentary that Confucian-Tang poetic tradition reserves for the *bĭxīng* device.

*The Three Officers* (三吏: 新安吏, 石壕吏, 潼關吏, all 759): three ancient-style verses composed during Du Fu's journey from Luoyang to Hua Province after the loyalist defeat at the Battle of Yecheng (鄴城, 759). Each poem documents an encounter with a Tang military officer conscripting civilians for the war effort, and each integrates the witnessing of human suffering with the political-moral commentary on the failure of the Tang military leadership.

*The Three Departures* (三別: 新婚別, 垂老別, 無家別, all 759): three ancient-style verses composed in the same period and depicting respectively a newly-married couple parting as the husband is conscripted (新婚別), an elderly couple parting as the husband is conscripted despite his age (垂老別), and a returning soldier finding his home destroyed and his family scattered (無家別). The three poems exhaust the social-witness mode in three distinct human-relational configurations.

*Autumn Meditations* (秋興八首, *Qiūxìng bā shǒu*, 766): eight seven-character regulated verses (七律, *qī lǜ*) composed at Kuizhou (夔州) in the late autumn of 766, integrating the autobiographical-historical mode with the high formal-aesthetic mode. The cycle is widely regarded by classical-poetry scholars as the supreme achievement of the *qī lǜ* form in the entire Tang canon.

## Part V · The Three Temperaments and the Buddhist-Daoist-Confucian Substrate

### V.1 The Three Temperaments

The WANG-WEI framework identifies three distinct poetic temperaments at the highest manifestation:

**The Wang-Wei-esque Temperament**: introverted, contemplative, integrated across poetry-painting-music-Buddhist-meditative practice. Wang Wei lived in dual mode for most of his adult life — the senior bureaucratic position at Chang'an and the contemplative retreat at the Wangchuan estate — and his courtesy name Mojie (摩詰, derived from Vimalakīrti) explicitly identified him with the lay-Buddhist householder-bodhisattva tradition that integrates contemplative practice with worldly responsibility. The Wang-Wei-esque temperament is characterised by *jingjie* (境界), *kong* (空) emptiness, the *shanshui* (山水) mountain-and-water mode, and the *wenrenhua* (文人畫) literati-painting integration. The temperament's signature emotional register is the contemplative — the still, the empty, the transcendentally-clear.

**The Li-Bai-esque Temperament**: extroverted, peripatetic, integrated across wine-and-friendship-and-Daoist-immortality-quest. Li Bai lived as the wandering visionary par excellence, with no fixed residence after 744, no bureaucratic post, no settled family life (he married at least twice and had several children but his domestic arrangements were chronically unstable), and a near-constant pursuit of the Daoist-immortality tradition through alchemical experimentation, mountain-climbing, and meditation. The Li-Bai-esque temperament is characterised by *qing* (情) emotion, *shénsī* (神思) spirit-thought, the wine-and-moon thematic, and the visionary-Daoist transcendence. The temperament's signature emotional register is the visionary — the imaginative, the transcendent, the immortality-questing.

**The Du-Fu-esque Temperament**: civic-engaged, autobiographical-historical, integrated across the Confucian-moral-political tradition with the personal-relational substrate. Du Fu lived as the Confucian moral-historical witness par excellence, with continuous civic engagement (failed examination attempts, brief imperial service, defence of the disgraced minister Fang Guan, advocacy for the suffering civilian population during the An Lushan rebellion), continuous documentation of the human cost of historical events, and continuous integration of the autobiographical with the historical. The Du-Fu-esque temperament is characterised by *shī shǐ* (詩史) poet-history, *bĭxīng* (比興) moral-political imagery, the social-witness mode, and the Confucian-conscientious tradition. The temperament's signature emotional register is the conscientious — the morally engaged, the historically aware, the human-suffering-witnessing.

### V.2 The Buddhist-Daoist-Confucian Tripartite Substrate

The eleventh-century Northern Song re-evaluation (Su Shi, Huang Tingjian, Wang Anshi, Ouyang Xiu) established the canonical identification of Wang Wei, Li Bai, and Du Fu as representing respectively the Buddhist, Daoist, and Confucian strands of Chinese culture. The WANG-WEI framework's identification of the tripartite substrate mapping onto the Contemplation-Vision-Conscience closure:

- **Buddhist substrate** (Wang Wei's mode): the Mahāyāna Buddhist tradition that emphasises emptiness (*śūnyatā* / 空 *kōng*), non-self (*anātman* / 無我 *wú wǒ*), and the dissolution of the perceiving-self into non-dual awareness. Maps onto the **Contemplation axis** of the CVC closure.
- **Daoist substrate** (Li Bai's mode): the philosophical-and-religious Daoist tradition that emphasises the *Dao* (道) as the spontaneous principle of nature, the *zhēn rén* (真人, "true person") as the sage who has integrated with the *Dao*, and the *xiāo yáo* (逍遙, "free wandering") as the mode of transcendent imaginative flight. Maps onto the **Vision axis** of the CVC closure.
- **Confucian substrate** (Du Fu's mode): the moral-historical-political Confucian tradition that emphasises *rén* (仁, "humaneness"), *yì* (義, "righteousness"), the *jūnzǐ* (君子, "exemplary person") as the moral exemplar, and the historical-witness function of literature as the foundation of political reform. Maps onto the **Conscience axis** of the CVC closure.

The three substrates do not compete in the Tang context; they coexist within the broader cultural framework that the late-Tang and Song critics named *sān jiào* (三教, "three teachings") — the integrated tripartite philosophical-religious system within which educated Tang officials were expected to be conversant with all three traditions. Wang Wei was an officially employed Tang official with a deep lay-Buddhist practice; Li Bai was a Daoist initiate (he formally received Daoist ordination in 744) with intense Confucian political ambitions; Du Fu was a deeply Confucian moralist with substantial Daoist-and-Buddhist sympathies (his poems frequently invoke Buddhist temples, Daoist mountains, and meditation practices). The tripartite substrate IS the cultural framework within which the three giants operated, and the CVC closure IS the operational realisation of this tripartite substrate at the universal-poetry-cognition scale.
